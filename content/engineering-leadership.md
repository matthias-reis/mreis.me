# On Engineering Leadership

After two decades building web platforms, the hardest problems were never
technical.

## The Myth of the 10x Engineer

The most impactful engineers I've worked with weren't the ones who wrote the
cleverest code — they were the ones who made everyone around them more
effective. They wrote clear documentation. They flagged blockers early. They
turned their solutions into patterns others could reuse.

Leverage is the real multiplier.

## Architecture Is a Social Contract

A design system isn't a library. It's an agreement between teams. A monorepo
isn't just a file structure — it's a statement about how work should flow across
an organization. The technical decisions are easy. Getting 40 contributors to
actually follow them is the hard part.

This means engineering leadership is communication work first, technical work
second. You're not writing the architecture — you're writing the narrative that
makes the architecture inevitable.

## Kaizen at Scale

Small, continuous improvements compound. A 1% reduction in CI time, multiplied
by 200 builds per day, multiplied by 12 months, is enormous. The teams I've seen
struggle most are the ones waiting for the "big rewrite" that will fix
everything. It never comes. The teams that win make the bar a little higher
every sprint.

## What I Look For in Engineers

- Do they leave code cleaner than they found it?
- Do they raise issues before they become crises?
- Do they ask "why" before asking "how"?
- Do they make the pull request easy to review?

The last one is underrated. Reviewability is a form of respect for your
colleagues' time.

## Closing Thought

Engineering leadership isn't about knowing all the answers. It's about building
the environment where the right questions get asked — and the people who know
the answers feel safe to share them.
